Different Types of Bank Accounts

Savings Account

  • This is the most popular type of account, ideal for everyday banking and saving money.

  • It offers interest on your deposits, although the rate may be low.

  • Savings accounts come with a debit card for easy access to your funds and usually allow a certain number of free transactions per month.

  • They may require you to maintain a minimum balance to avoid charges.

Current Account

  • Designed for businesses and individuals with frequent deposits and withdrawals.

  • Current accounts typically don't earn interest and may have higher minimum balance requirements than savings accounts.

  • Suitable for parking your money for a fixed period to earn a higher interest rate than a savings account.

  • They may offer features like overdraft facilities, which allow you to withdraw more than what's currently in your account, subject to a limit and fees.

Fixed Deposit Account (FD)

  • The term of an FD can range from a few weeks to several years.

  • You cannot withdraw your money before the maturity date without penalty.

Recurring Deposit Account (RD)

  • This account helps you inculcate a regular savings habit.

  • You earn interest on the total amount deposited at the end of the term.

  • You deposit a fixed sum of money into your RD account every month for a predetermined period.

Salary Account

  • Offered by many banks in collaboration with companies to credit their employees' salaries.

  • Salary accounts may come with benefits like waived minimum balance requirements, free debit cards, and discounts on other bank products.

NRI Account

  • Meant for Non-Resident Indians (NRIs) who want to maintain a bank account in India.

  • NRI accounts allow you to hold foreign currency and rupees, send money to India, and invest in certain financial instruments.

Demat Account

  • Not technically a bank account, but a demat account is linked to your bank savings account and is required to hold shares and other financial securities in electronic form.

  • You can use a demat account to buy, sell, and hold stocks, mutual funds, and bonds.
