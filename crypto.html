What is Cryptocurrency? Cryptocurrency is a type of digital or virtual currency that uses cryptography for secure transactions and to control the creation of new units. Unlike traditional currencies issued by governments (such as dollars or euros), cryptocurrencies operate independently of any central authority or government.

Why was it introduced? Cryptocurrency was introduced as a decentralised alternative to traditional forms of money. It was created to address some of the limitations and shortcomings of traditional banking and financial systems, such as:

  • CentralizationTraditional banking systems are centralised, meaning they are controlled by a single authority, such as a government or central bank. Cryptocurrency, on the other hand, is decentralised, meaning it is not controlled by any single entity. This decentralisation aims to eliminate the need for intermediaries, reduce the risk of censorship or manipulation, and give users more control over their money.

  • Global AccessibilityCryptocurrency transactions can be conducted globally, without the need for traditional banking infrastructure or intermediaries. This enables fast and low-cost cross-border transactions, making cryptocurrency particularly useful for remittances and international payments.

  • Financial InclusionCryptocurrency has the potential to provide access to financial services for individuals who are underserved or excluded by traditional banking systems. Anyone with internet access can participate in the cryptocurrency ecosystem, regardless of their location or socioeconomic status.

  • Inflation ResistanceCentral banks have the authority to control the value of traditional currencies by adjusting interest rates, implementing quantitative easing measures, or printing more money. Unlike traditional currencies, which are controlled by central authorities like governments or central banks, cryptocurrencies operate on decentralized networks powered by blockchain technology. For example, Bitcoin has a fixed supply cap of 21 million coins.

However, it still has certain disadvantages:

  • Irreversible Transactions:Cryptocurrency transactions are irreversible once executed, unlike traditional banking transactions that can be cancelled or reversed. Users must exercise caution to avoid mistakes or fraudulent transactions, as there's no recourse for recovering lost funds. Unlike traditional banking transactions, where financial institutions act as intermediaries and can reverse transactions under certain circumstances (such as in cases of fraud or error), cryptocurrency transactions don't involve intermediaries. Instead, they are peer-to-peer transactions between anonymous users. For example, if you accidentally send cryptocurrency to the wrong wallet address, there's no way to retrieve the funds because you don't have any personal information about the recipient, and they don't have any information about you.

  • Volatility in Value:Cryptocurrency values can be highly volatile, experiencing significant fluctuations in price over short periods. Unlike traditional currencies, which are backed by governments, cryptocurrency values are determined by market demand and speculation, leading to price instability.

  • Not a legal tender: Legal tender status means that a currency is officially recognized and accepted as a medium of exchange for goods, services, and debts within a country's jurisdiction. While cryptocurrencies can be used as a medium of exchange in some cases, they lack legal tender status. This means that businesses and individuals are not required to accept cryptocurrencies as payment, and there may be limited acceptance or infrastructure for using cryptocurrencies in everyday transactions. As a result, cryptocurrency users may face challenges in finding merchants or service providers willing to accept cryptocurrency payments, limiting its practical utility as a form of currency for day-to-day transactions.
